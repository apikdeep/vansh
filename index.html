Pollution

pollution refers to the introduction of harmful or unwanted substances into the environment, adversely affecting ecosystems, human health, and quality of life. It encompasses various forms such as air pollution (from emissions, smog), water pollution (from contaminants, sewage), soil pollution (from chemicals)

Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances such as chemicals, toxins, and waste contaminate water bodies like rivers, lakes, and oceans. Sources include industrial effluents, agricultural runoff, and improper waste disposal. It poses threats to aquatic life, ecosystems, and human health, impacting drinking water quality and biodiversity. Mitigation efforts involve regulation, treatment facilities, and sustainable practices to preserve water resources and protect the environment.

Types of Water Pollution

  • Chemical Pollution : Chemical water pollution stems from contaminants like pesticides, heavy metals, and industrial chemicals entering water bodies. These pollutants disrupt ecosystems, harm aquatic life, and endanger human health through consumption or contact. Agricultural runoff, improper waste disposal, and industrial discharges are primary sources, necessitating stringent regulations and remediation efforts.
  • Biological Pollution : Biological pollution refers to the introduction of harmful organisms or their byproducts into ecosystems, impacting biodiversity and human health. Examples include invasive species disrupting native habitats, pathogens contaminating water sources, and pollutants fostering algal blooms. Biological pollution can result from human activities like transportation, agriculture, and waste disposal, necessitating measures to control and mitigate its effects.
  • Sediment Pollution : Sediment pollution occurs when soil, sand, or silt is washed or carried into water bodies, altering aquatic habitats and ecosystems. It often results from erosion caused by human activities such as construction, agriculture, and deforestation. Sediment can smother aquatic life, impair water quality, and degrade habitats, necessitating erosion control measures and watershed management strategies.
  • Nutrient Pollution : Nutrient pollution arises from excessive amounts of nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus entering water bodies, typically from agricultural runoff, sewage discharge, and fertilizers. These nutrients fuel algae growth, leading to harmful algal blooms. As algae die and decompose, oxygen levels decrease, causing "dead zones" where aquatic life can't survive. Nutrient pollution poses risks to ecosystems, water quality, and human health, requiring nutrient management practices and wastewater treatment.
  • Thermal Pollution : Thermal pollution occurs when human activities such as industrial processes, power generation, or urbanization elevate water temperatures in natural water bodies. Increased temperatures can harm aquatic organisms sensitive to temperature changes, disrupt ecosystems, and decrease dissolved oxygen levels. Thermal pollution often results from the discharge of heated water into rivers, lakes, or oceans, necessitating regulations and cooling technologies to mitigate its impact.
  • Radioactive Pollution : radioactive pollution arises from the release of radioactive substances into the environment, often from nuclear power plants, mining operations, or industrial processes. These substances emit ionizing radiation, posing significant health risks to humans and ecosystems. Accidental releases or improper disposal of radioactive waste can contaminate air, water, and soil, necessitating stringent regulations, monitoring, and cleanup efforts to mitigate hazards and protect public health.

study from 2022, published in Chemosphere, analysed the amount, types and sources of microplastics in the sediment of Anchar lake in Srinagar. It evaluated 24 samples under a microscope and used a technique called Attenuated Total Reflectance Fourier Transform Infrared (ATR-FTIR) spectroscopy to observe their composition. “The results showed the most common type of plastic found in the sediment was polyamide (PA), which accounted for 96%, followed by polyethylene terephthalate (PET) at 1.4%, polystyrene (PS) at 1.4%, polyvinyl chloride (PVC) at 0.9%, and polypropylene (PP) at 0.7%,” the study stated.

With growing water scarcity, jeopardized by climate change, springs are likely to perform a vital role in meeting the domestic water demand in future. This paper examines the water quality status of Kashmir valley springs in relation to their geographical location, regional hydrogeological conditions, anthropogenic activities and climate change. We analyzed data for 258 springs from the whole Kashmir valley using water quality index (WQI) and geographic information system techniques. WQI ranged from 23 (excellent water) to 537 (water unsuitable for drinking). The WQI indicated that 39.5% of the springs had excellent waters, 47.7% had good water, 5% had poor water, 1.6% had very-poor water, and 6.2% of the springs had water unfit for drinking purposes. The Piper diagram identified Ca–Mg–HCO3, Ca–Mg–SO4, and Na–HCO3 as the most predominant hydro-chemical facies, whereas Gibbs diagram revealed that the water of springs in the study region is mainly controlled by rock weathering dominance. The results of the study offer inputs about the water quality to be used by the concerned departments and agencies at a bigger scale for drinking purposes. Our findings therefore suggest that springs which are in thousands in Kashmir landscape have the potential to offer viable solution to the rising drinking water demand and therefore merit an attention for their protection and management.

Water pollution status in Bihar

last update in January 2022, water pollution in Bihar was a significant concern, particularly in areas with high population density, industrial activity, and inadequate sanitation infrastructure. The Ganga River, which flows through Bihar, faces pollution from various sources, including untreated sewage, industrial effluents, and agricultural runoff. The government has undertaken initiatives to address water pollution, including the Namami Gange program aimed at cleaning and rejuvenating the Ganga. However, challenges persist due to limited resources, enforcement gaps, and ongoing urbanization and industrialization.
